# Cybersecurity

## Overview

Cybersecurity is the discipline of protecting information, software, hardware, networks, cloud platforms, cyber-physical systems, and digital infrastructure from unauthorized access, misuse, disruption, and cyber attacks.

Modern organizations depend on secure digital systems to operate businesses, governments, research laboratories, healthcare systems, financial institutions, manufacturing facilities, transportation networks, and critical infrastructure. As computing technologies evolve from classical systems toward Artificial Intelligence (AI), Quantum Computing, and future intelligent ecosystems, cybersecurity must also evolve to address new threats and opportunities.

Within the FAEP Academy, cybersecurity is treated as an engineering discipline that spans the complete technology lifecycle—from secure system design and software development to enterprise governance, cloud security, AI security, quantum-safe cryptography, and future QAI security architectures.

---

# Security Fundamentals

Modern cybersecurity is built upon a set of fundamental engineering principles that protect information, computing systems, communication networks, applications, and digital infrastructure throughout their lifecycle.

Rather than treating security as an isolated activity, modern engineering integrates security into system design, software development, deployment, operations, and continuous monitoring.

The FAEP Academy adopts a **Security by Design** philosophy, ensuring that cybersecurity is considered from the earliest stages of engineering and remains an integral part of system operations.

---

# Core Security Principles

The foundation of cybersecurity is often described using the CIA Triad.

## Confidentiality

Confidentiality ensures that information is accessible only to authorized users and systems.

Examples include:

- Encryption
- Identity and Access Management (IAM)
- Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)
- Role-Based Access Control (RBAC)
- Attribute-Based Access Control (ABAC)

---

## Integrity

Integrity ensures that information remains accurate, complete, and trustworthy throughout its lifecycle.

Examples include:

- Digital Signatures
- Checksums
- Hash Functions
- Secure Version Control
- Immutable Logging

---

## Availability

Availability ensures that systems and information remain accessible when required.

Examples include:

- High Availability
- Disaster Recovery
- Backup Systems
- Fault Tolerance
- Load Balancing
- Distributed Architectures

---

# Authentication

Authentication verifies the identity of users, devices, applications, and services.

Common authentication methods include:

- Username and Password
- Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)
- Certificates
- Biometrics
- Hardware Security Keys
- Single Sign-On (SSO)
- Federated Identity

---

# Authorization

Authorization determines what authenticated users are permitted to access.

Common authorization models include:

- Role-Based Access Control (RBAC)
- Attribute-Based Access Control (ABAC)
- Policy-Based Access Control (PBAC)
- Least Privilege Principle
- Just-in-Time Access

---

# Accountability

Modern enterprise systems require complete traceability of operations.

This includes:

- Logging
- Auditing
- Digital Forensics
- Activity Monitoring
- Compliance Reporting
- Security Event Correlation

---

# Cloud Security

## Overview

Cloud computing has transformed the way organizations design, deploy, and operate digital services. Modern enterprises increasingly rely on public cloud, private cloud, hybrid cloud, edge computing, and multi-cloud environments to support business operations, research, and innovation.

While cloud platforms provide scalability, resilience, and flexibility, they also introduce new security challenges related to identity, data protection, workload isolation, supply chain security, compliance, and shared responsibility.

Cloud Security protects cloud-based infrastructure, applications, services, data, and users while maintaining compliance with organizational and regulatory requirements.

# Quantum Security and Post-Quantum Cryptography

## Overview

Quantum computing represents one of the most significant technological advances in modern computing. While fault-tolerant quantum computers promise breakthroughs in scientific discovery, optimization, chemistry, materials science, and artificial intelligence, they also introduce new cybersecurity challenges.

Several widely used public-key cryptographic algorithms rely on mathematical problems that are believed to be difficult for classical computers but may become vulnerable to sufficiently capable quantum computers.

Quantum Security focuses on protecting information, communication systems, computing platforms, and digital infrastructure against both current and future quantum-enabled threats.

Within the FAEP Academy, Quantum Security complements traditional cybersecurity by preparing organizations for a gradual transition toward quantum-safe computing environments.

---

Modern digital infrastructure depends upon cryptographic algorithms for:

- Identity Management
- Authentication
- Digital Signatures
- Secure Communications
- Software Distribution
- Financial Transactions
- Cloud Services
- Government Infrastructure

Quantum computing may significantly impact some of these cryptographic techniques in the future.

Organizations should therefore begin planning long-term migration strategies.

- Enterprise Migration Planning
