They bridge the gap between Information Engineering, Metadata, Taxonomy, Engineering Memory, Artificial Intelligence, Enterprise Engineering, and future Quantum Artificial Intelligence (QAI) systems.

Knowledge Graphs transform isolated information into connected organizational intelligence.

# Knowledge Graph Foundations

## Overview

Knowledge Graphs provide a structured method for representing knowledge by connecting entities through meaningful relationships. Rather than storing isolated pieces of information, Knowledge Graphs organize knowledge into interconnected networks that capture the semantics of real-world concepts and their interactions.

Traditional information systems typically focus on storing data within tables, documents, or files. While these approaches effectively manage information, they often make it difficult to understand the relationships between engineering artifacts, business processes, research activities, and enterprise knowledge.

Knowledge Graphs address this challenge by explicitly representing both entities and the relationships that connect them. This enables richer information discovery, semantic reasoning, intelligent search, traceability, and knowledge reuse.

Within the FAEP Academy, Knowledge Graphs represent one of the core technologies supporting Information-Centric Engineering. They provide the semantic foundation for Engineering Memory, Artificial Intelligence, Digital Twins, Enterprise Engineering, and future Quantum Artificial Intelligence (QAI) platforms.

# What is a Knowledge Graph?

A Knowledge Graph is a network of interconnected entities linked together through meaningful semantic relationships.

# Graph Theory Fundamentals

## Overview

Graph Theory provides the mathematical foundation for Knowledge Graphs. It offers a structured way to model complex systems by representing real-world objects as interconnected nodes and relationships.

Although Graph Theory originated in mathematics, it has become an essential discipline in computer science, engineering, artificial intelligence, networking, cybersecurity, logistics, social networks, and enterprise information systems.

Within the FAEP Academy, Graph Theory is introduced from an engineering perspective. Rather than focusing on mathematical formalism, the emphasis is on understanding how graphs represent engineering knowledge, system dependencies, business relationships, and organizational intelligence.

Graph Theory provides the structural foundation upon which modern Knowledge Graphs are built.

---

# What is a Graph?

A graph is a collection of nodes connected by edges.

```text
Node ---- Edge ---- Node
```

More generally,

```text
      A
     / \
    B---C
     \
      D
```

A graph represents objects and the relationships between them.

---

# Nodes (Vertices)

Nodes represent entities.

Examples include:

- Engineer
- Customer
- Product
- Requirement
- Software Module
- Sensor
- Digital Twin
- Project
- Research Paper

Each node represents something meaningful within the engineering domain.

---

# Edges (Relationships)

Edges connect nodes.

Examples include:

- Uses
- Designs
- Owns
- Depends On
- Implements
- Tests
- Controls
- Validates
- Reports To

Edges describe how entities interact.

Without relationships, a graph becomes merely a collection of isolated objects.

# Directed Graphs


Examples include:

```text

```

- Collaborates With

Undirected graphs represent mutual relationships.

---

Examples include:

- Confidence


Weights enable optimization and analytical reasoning.

# Semantic Relationships

## Overview

Semantic Relationships describe how entities are meaningfully connected within a Knowledge Graph. They transform simple graph structures into intelligent knowledge networks capable of supporting information discovery, reasoning, engineering traceability, and decision support.

While Graph Theory explains how nodes are connected, Semantic Relationships explain what those connections mean.

Within the FAEP Academy, Semantic Relationships provide the foundation for Information-Centric Engineering by enabling Engineering Memory, Knowledge Graphs, Artificial Intelligence (AI), Digital Twins, Enterprise Engineering, and future Quantum Artificial Intelligence (QAI) systems to understand and reason about engineering knowledge.

Semantic meaning transforms connected information into connected intelligence.

---

# What is a Semantic Relationship?

A Semantic Relationship describes the meaning of the connection between two entities.

Example:

```text
Engineer
      │ Designs
      ▼
Subsystem
```

The relationship **Designs** has engineering meaning.

Replacing it with:

```text
Engineer
      │ Owns
      ▼
Subsystem
```

changes the interpretation completely.

The entities remain identical.

The relationship changes the meaning.

# Ontologies and Semantic Models

## Overview

Ontologies provide the conceptual foundation that enables Knowledge Graphs to represent information with shared meaning. They define the concepts, categories, properties, relationships, and rules that describe a particular domain of knowledge.

While Knowledge Graphs store connected knowledge, ontologies define how that knowledge should be understood.

Within the FAEP Academy, ontologies provide a common vocabulary that allows Engineering Memory, Artificial Intelligence (AI), Digital Twins, Enterprise Engineering, and future Quantum Artificial Intelligence (QAI) systems to communicate consistently across organizational boundaries.

Ontologies enable different people, applications, and intelligent systems to interpret information in the same way.

---

# What is an Ontology?

An ontology is a formal description of a knowledge domain.

It defines:

- Concepts
- Categories
- Relationships
- Properties
- Constraints
- Rules
- Shared Vocabulary

An ontology answers questions such as:

- What things exist?
- How are they related?
- What characteristics do they have?
- What rules govern them?

Rather than storing information, an ontology defines the structure and meaning of knowledge.

---

# Why Ontologies Matter

Organizations often use different terminology for similar concepts.

For example:

```text
Customer
Client
Consumer
Account
```

Although different departments may use different terms, an ontology establishes a common understanding.

Shared meaning improves:

- Collaboration
- Integration
- Search
- Automation
- Artificial Intelligence
- Decision Support

---

# Ontology versus Taxonomy

These concepts are often confused.

A taxonomy organizes concepts into classifications.

Example:

```text

    │
────────────
│          │
Car      Truck
```

An ontology goes further.

```text
Vehicle
      │
Manufactured By
      │
Company
      │
Owned By
      │
Customer
      │
Operated By
      │
Driver
```

Taxonomies classify.

Ontologies explain meaning and relationships.

---

# Ontology versus Metadata

Metadata describes information.

Example:

```text

Version
Date
Status
```

An ontology defines the meaning behind the information.

Example:

```text
Requirement
      │
Implemented By
      ▼
Subsystem
```

Metadata describes.

Ontologies define.

Both are complementary.

---

# Ontology versus Knowledge Graph

An ontology is not the Knowledge Graph.

The ontology defines the model.

The Knowledge Graph contains actual knowledge.

Example:

```text

```

Knowledge Graph

```text

Designs
Navigation System
```

One defines the vocabulary.

The other stores connected knowledge.

# Glossary of Terms

This glossary introduces key concepts used throughout this tutorial. The definitions are intentionally technology-neutral and emphasize engineering principles that remain applicable across different platforms and implementation approaches.

---

## Artificial Intelligence (AI)

Artificial Intelligence is the field of computer science focused on developing systems capable of performing tasks that typically require human intelligence, including learning, reasoning, decision support, language understanding, and pattern recognition.

---

## Class

A class is a category or type of entity within an ontology. Classes define common characteristics shared by groups of related entities.

Example:

```text
Vehicle
    ├── Car
    ├── Truck
    └── Bus
```

---

## Digital Twin

A Digital Twin is a digital representation of a physical asset, process, system, or organization that supports monitoring, simulation, analysis, and lifecycle management.

Knowledge Graphs provide semantic context for Digital Twins.

---

## Engineering Memory

Engineering Memory is the structured preservation of engineering knowledge, including requirements, architectures, design decisions, lessons learned, validation results, operational experience, and historical information that supports continuous engineering.

---

## Entity

An entity represents a uniquely identifiable object, concept, person, organization, process, component, or event within a Knowledge Graph.

Examples include:

- Customer
- Product
- Requirement
- Engineer
- Sensor

---

## Explainable AI (XAI)

Explainable AI refers to AI systems that provide understandable reasoning, evidence, or traceability for their outputs and decisions.

Knowledge Graphs improve explainability by preserving semantic relationships.

---

## Graph

A graph is a mathematical structure consisting of nodes connected by edges.

Knowledge Graphs extend graph structures by assigning semantic meaning to these relationships.

---

## Graph Database

A graph database is a database designed to store highly connected information using graph structures consisting of nodes, relationships, and properties.

---

## Information Engineering

Information Engineering is the discipline of organizing, managing, integrating, and governing information to support engineering, business, and scientific activities.

---

## Knowledge Base

A Knowledge Base is a structured repository containing information that supports reasoning, search, decision-making, or expert systems.

Knowledge Graphs represent an advanced form of knowledge organization.

---

## Knowledge Graph

A Knowledge Graph is a semantic representation of connected entities and their relationships that enables knowledge discovery, contextual understanding, intelligent search, and reasoning.

---

## Knowledge Representation

Knowledge Representation is the process of organizing information into structures that enable humans and intelligent systems to interpret, reason about, and utilize knowledge.

---

## Metadata

Metadata is structured information that describes other information.

Examples include:

- Author
- Version
- Creation Date
- Owner
- Status
- Classification

---

## Node

A node represents an entity within a graph.

Examples include:

- Person
- Organization
- Product
- Requirement

---

## Ontology

An ontology defines the concepts, classes, relationships, constraints, and vocabulary that describe a knowledge domain.

Ontologies provide semantic meaning for Knowledge Graphs.

---

## Property

A property describes an attribute associated with an entity or relationship.

Examples include:

- Name
- Version
- Priority
- Status
- Owner

---

## QAI (Quantum Artificial Intelligence)

Within the FAEP Academy, QAI represents the integration of Artificial Intelligence, Information Engineering, Engineering Memory, Digital Twins, and hybrid classical–quantum computing to support future intelligent engineering ecosystems.

---

## Relationship

A relationship connects two entities and describes how they are associated.

Example:

```text
Engineer
      │ Designs
      ▼
Subsystem
```

---

## Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)

Retrieval-Augmented Generation combines information retrieval with Large Language Models by supplying relevant organizational knowledge before generating responses.

Knowledge Graphs can enhance RAG by providing trusted semantic context.

---

## Semantic Search

Semantic Search retrieves information based on meaning, context, and relationships rather than exact keyword matching.

---

## Semantic Model

A semantic model describes concepts and relationships within a particular domain, enabling consistent interpretation of information.

---

## Taxonomy

A taxonomy is a hierarchical classification system used to organize information into categories and subcategories.

Example:

```text
Engineering
    ├── Mechanical
    ├── Electrical
    └── Software
```

---

## Traceability

Traceability is the ability to follow relationships between information throughout its lifecycle.

Engineering traceability commonly connects:

Requirements → Design → Implementation → Testing → Operations

---

## Triple

A triple is a fundamental representation used by many semantic technologies.

A triple consists of:

```text
Subject
Predicate
Object
```

Example:

```text
Engineer
Designs
Subsystem
```

---

## Vector Database

A Vector Database stores numerical vector representations of information to support similarity search and AI applications.

Vector databases and Knowledge Graphs often complement one another in modern AI architectures.

---

## Vocabulary

A vocabulary is a standardized collection of terms used consistently within a particular domain.

Controlled vocabularies improve semantic consistency across organizations.
